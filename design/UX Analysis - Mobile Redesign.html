Phase 1 — UX Analysis

The desktop tool is a study app.

01 What a player actually does at the table

Design for frequency, not for the feature list

You asked the right question: what does a poker player do most? Everything else follows from the answer. Here is every at-the-table action ranked by how often it happens in a session. The top three happen on every single hand — they get the thumb zone, the biggest targets, and zero scrolling. The bottom of the ladder is real, but it belongs in Study, not in the player's face during a hand.

Principle

If it doesn't happen on rungs 1–4, it does not get permanent screen space in Play. The mistake in the current app is treating all seven as peers in one scrolling column.

02 Optimizing the flow for one hand

Invert the screen around the thumb

Held one-handed, the thumb sweeps a comfortable arc across the bottom two-thirds of the screen. The top corners are a stretch; the very top is a two-hand reach. The current app puts navigation and all the controls up top and the Analyze button 1,000px down — exactly backwards.

  • Navigation drops to the bottom. A tab bar in the thumb arc replaces four stacked text tabs up top — and reclaims ~50px of vertical space at the same time.
  • Output rises, input sits low. The action badge pins near the top where the eye lands; the card entry and New Hand live where the thumb already is.
  • Config collapses into a pull-up sheet. Position, pot, stack live in a bottom sheet you thumb open and dismiss — never a scroll past them to reach the cards.
  • Auto-analyze stays. No separate Analyze tap on mobile — the result updates as cards land. Preserve the 300ms debounce.

03 Information architecture

Four equal tabs → two clear intents

Scenario Builder and Play Advisor secretly do the same job — configure a hand, get analysis — at two levels of abstraction. On a phone that overlap is just confusion and wasted chrome. The code already tracks state.mode = 'live' | 'study'; the IA should simply make that split visible.

Today · 4 peers
  • Scenario Builder (default — wrong for mobile)
  • Play Advisor (live + study buried inside)
  • Probability Matrix (unreadable on phone)
  • Hand Journal (empty placeholder)
Proposed · bottom tab bar
  • Play — default on mobile. The Live table surface.
  • Study — sub-tabs: Advisor · Scenarios · Matrix
  • Journal — saved hands & session review
  • AI Coach — a sheet reachable from both, not a tab
Why three, not four

Three is the most you can put in a thumb-reachable bottom bar with icon + label and still leave breathing room. The Matrix earns its keep inside Study, where the user has time — not as a peer of the at-table flow. Desktop can keep the full four-tab layout; this restructure is mobile-first via breakpoints.

The current builder fires three side-by-side dropdowns, eight opponent buttons, a card grid, and toggles at the user before they've done anything. On a phone that's a wall. The fix is sequencing and disclosure:

  • Context becomes one summary row. Replace the three dropdowns with a single tappable line — "TAG · BTN · 6-max" — that opens one sheet to edit game, position, style, and table size together. Native segmented controls for the low-count choices; a wheel picker for the 6 styles with their descriptions.
  • Presets lead. They're the path 80% of users take, so they sit up top at full weight — not styled like afterthought tags.
  • Strategy Insight rises above the fold. The action badge + nut-potential bar is the most useful pre-analysis content; today it's buried under seven sections. It comes up right under the presets.
  • Custom builder is one collapsed row. "Build custom ⌄" expands the structure / rank / suitedness controls only for the power users who want them.
  • Cut the dev-facing noise. The pair:AA:ds query-preview block and redundant constraint tags are hidden on mobile.
05 Card entry — the most-touched control

Two big taps beat scanning 52 tiny ones

The 13-column grid renders ~28px buttons at 9.6px text on a 375px screen — below every Apple touch-target and legibility minimum. The loupe magnifier is a clever patch, but it asks the user to press-hold-drag-release over targets they can't read. Borrow the pattern every fast mobile poker tool uses:

  • Rank first, suit second. 13 big rank buttons (5-wide, ~60px), then 4 large suit buttons. Two deliberate taps per card, both in the thumb zone, both legible.
  • Auto-advance & auto-fill. Hole slots fill first, then board — the existing behavior, kept. Tap a filled slot to re-enter replace mode.
  • 4-color deck, fixed. Keep the four suit colors for fast recognition, but move diamonds off #2563eb so card blue never collides with UI blue.
  • Power-user shortcut. A "type it" field accepts AKQJs notation for players who'd rather thumb-type than tap. The grid stays available via a toggle.
  • Haptic tick on each pick in the iOS shell — confirmation without looking, which matters when the phone's under the rail.

81 cells of 10px numbers will never be a good phone experience, and squeezing harder only makes it worse. But a player never reads the whole grid — they read one row: "I have a flush; how likely is someone to beat it?" So we reframe the table as a focused, single-question view:

  • A color-only minimap up top keeps the whole 9×9 as pure heat swatches — no numbers — purely to orient and to tap into a different row.
  • The selected row becomes the screen. Each opponent hand type is a labeled, color-coded bar with its percentage, sorted by threat so the dangerous matchups rise to the top.
  • Swipe left/right to change your hand (the row); the bars re-sort. Tap a bar to drill into the Scenario Builder, preserving the existing click-through.
  • Live mode gets the one-number version — a single "danger meter" derived from the same row, no table at all.
  • This also fixes the contrast failures: big bars carry the color, and the number sits on the dark surface at full legibility instead of white-on-red at 10px.
07 The AI Play Advisor as a native chat

Make the Coach feel like Messages

Today the Coach is a fixed 400px panel that renders off the right edge of every phone — a hard break. Rebuilt as a full-height sheet that slides up over the Play/Study surface, it should read like iMessage, because that's the mental model users already have for chat:

  • Real bubbles. Coach left in a gray bubble with a tail, you right in a teal bubble; consecutive messages group. A three-dot typing indicator while it thinks, then text streams in.
  • The hand rides at the top. A pinned context chip shows the cards being discussed, so the conversation is always anchored to a specific spot.
  • Suggested replies kill the typing tax. Tappable chips — "What if the flop is monotone?", "How does position change this?" — sit above the keyboard. This is the fix for the #1 Coach friction: nobody wants to thumb-type strategy questions at a table.
  • Rich answers as card bubbles. The recommendation can arrive as a styled bubble (action + sizing) inline in the thread, not as a wall of text.
  • Input bar floats above the keyboard with a growing field and a round send button — standard iOS, with the BYOK key setup moved into a calm first-run sheet instead of a cramped modal.

The Journal is the "stickiness" feature — the thing that pulls a player back between sessions. The data model is already designed (IndexedDB, full advisor snapshot). The UI should make saved hands feel like a deck you flip through:

  • One-tap save from any result. The action badge gets a save affordance; the whole advisor snapshot is captured silently.
  • Grouped by session. Each session is a header with a one-line summary — "Tue · 12 hands · 4R 3F · 78% avg conf" — turning the Journal into a session log, not just a pile.
  • Each card shows what matters at a glance: the hand, the board, the recommendation badge, and — crucially — what you actually did vs. what was advised, flagged when they diverge.
  • Filter chips across the top: by tag (#bluff, #river), by action, by style, by date. Horizontal scroll, no menus.
  • Gestures do the work. Swipe a row to replay it in the Advisor (all fields pre-filled) or delete. Swipe-to-log — right "followed advice," left "deviated" — builds the record with zero typing.
  • An empty state that invites, not a "coming soon" sign — "Your saved hands will live here. Save your first from the Advisor."
09 What I need from you for Phase 2

Decisions before we go hi-fi

These wireframes are deliberately monochrome — structure, not skin. Once you're happy with the thinking above, Phase 2 builds it as a real, clickable iOS-feeling prototype in the dark poker aesthetic. A few forks I'd like your call on first:

A The palette

The brief documents #1a1a2e + teal #00d4aa; you mentioned #1e293b. I'm proposing a deep slate base with the teal as the single brand accent. Lock that, or explore a couple of accent directions?

B Card entry default

Make rank-then-suit the default picker (grid as a toggle), or keep the 52-grid as primary with bigger targets? I lean rank-then-suit for one-handed speed.

C Nav model

Three-item bottom bar (Play · Study · Journal) with Matrix inside Study — or do you want Matrix promoted to its own tab on phone?

D Scope of the prototype

Build the full Play flow first as one polished iPhone prototype, then add Study/Journal — or lay all five screens side-by-side on a canvas to compare directions?

My recommendation

Lock the teal-on-slate palette, default to rank-then-suit, three-tab bar, and let me build the Play flow end-to-end first — card entry → live result → New Hand → pull-up situation sheet — as a working iPhone prototype you can thumb through. That's the hand that gets played 1,000 times a session; nail it, then Study and Journal follow the same system.
